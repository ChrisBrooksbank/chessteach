# Sicilian Defense

**1.e4 c5**

The most popular and theoretically important response to 1.e4. Black fights for the centre asymmetrically, creating unbalanced positions where both sides have winning chances. Statistically, the Sicilian gives Black the best results against 1.e4.

## The Open Sicilian (2.Nf3 and 3.d4)

After **2.Nf3** followed by **3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4**, White opens the position and gets central space. Black gets the semi-open c-file and an asymmetric pawn structure that creates dynamic play.

---

## Najdorf Variation (5...a6)

```
1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6
```

The "Rolls Royce" of chess openings. 5...a6 prepares ...e5 or ...b5 and prevents Nb5/Bb5+. Incredibly rich in theory and ideas.

### Key Lines After 5...a6

| White's 6th | Name | Character |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| 6.Bg5 | Classical/Najdorf Main | Extremely sharp; ...e6, ...Be7 |
| 6.Be3 | English Attack | White castles queenside; mutual attacks |
| 6.Be2 | Positional | Slower; manoeuvring play |
| 6.f3 | English Attack (modern) | Prepares Be3, Qd2, O-O-O |

### The Poisoned Pawn

```
6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6 8.Qd2 Qxb2
```

Black grabs the b2 pawn — one of the most analysed and dangerous lines in all of chess. White gets a massive attack; Black must navigate precisely.

### Strategic Ideas

| White | Black |
|-------|-------|
| Central space with e4; kingside or central attack | Counter on the queenside with ...b5, ...Bb7 |
| f4–f5 break to open the kingside | ...d5 or ...e5 breaks to challenge the centre |
| The English Attack: Be3, f3, Qd2, O-O-O, g4 | ...Rc8, ...b5, ...Rxc3 exchange sacrifice |

### Famous Practitioners

Bobby Fischer, Garry Kasparov (lifetime devotee), Maxime Vachier-Lagrave, Hikaru Nakamura.

### Famous Games

- Fischer vs Spassky, 1972 WCh Game 6 — Fischer's legendary win as Black
- Kasparov's many Najdorf battles

## Dragon Variation (5...g6)

```
1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 g6
```

Black fianchettoes the bishop to g7, creating a "dragon" formation. The Yugoslav Attack (6.Be3 Bg7 7.f3 O-O 8.Qd2 Nc6 9.Bc4 Bd7 10.O-O-O) leads to some of the sharpest positions in chess.

### Yugoslav Attack — Main Line

```
10.O-O-O Rc8 11.Bb3 Ne5 12.h4 h5
```

Both sides attack the opposing king. Speed is everything — one tempo can decide the game.

### Key Tactical Themes

- **The exchange sacrifice ...Rxc3** — perhaps the most important motif in the Dragon
- Mutual king hunts
- h-file attacks for White
- The Bg7's power on the long diagonal
- See [Tactics — Discovered Attacks](../../tactics/discovered-attacks.md) and [Attacking — Opposite-Side Castling](../../middlegame/attacking-the-king.md)

### Famous Practitioners

Garry Kasparov (as a junior), Teimour Radjabov, Hikaru Nakamura.

## Sveshnikov Variation (5...e5)

```
1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 e5
6.Ndb5 d6 7.Bg5 a6 8.Na3 b5 9.Bxf6 gxf6 10.Nd5 f5
```

Black plays ...e5, creating a hole on d5 but gaining central space. Despite the ugly doubled f-pawns after Bxf6 gxf6, Black gets the bishop pair and dynamic compensation.

### Strategic Ideas

| White | Black |
|-------|-------|
| Permanent knight on d5 — extremely powerful | Bishop pair and dynamic piece play |
| Exploit doubled f-pawns and weakened kingside | ...f5 break activates the position |
| Prophylactic play to contain Black's activity | ...b5 advance gains queenside space |

### Famous Practitioners

Evgeny Sveshnikov (pioneer), Vladimir Kramnik, Magnus Carlsen.

## Scheveningen Variation (5...e6)

```
1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 e6
```

The "small centre" setup — flexible and solid. Black keeps options open.

### The Keres Attack (6.g4!?)

An aggressive attempt to punish Black's e6/d6 structure before full development. Very sharp.

### Famous Practitioners

Kasparov, Karpov (as Black), Anand.

## Other Sicilian Variations

### Accelerated Dragon (4...g6)
Avoids the Yugoslav Attack by playing ...g6 before ...d6. White's critical test is the **Maroczy Bind** (5.c4) — clamping down with c4 + e4.

### Kan Variation (4...a6)
Maximum flexibility — Black can transpose to Scheveningen, Taimanov, or unique positions.

### Taimanov Variation (4...Nc6)
```
5.Nc3 Qc7 6.Be3 a6 7.Qd2 Nf6 8.O-O-O
```
Structured play with flexibility. Famous practitioners: Mark Taimanov, Magnus Carlsen.

### Classical Sicilian (5...Nc6, then 6.Bg5 — Richter-Rauzer Attack)
Very aggressive from White's side with Bg5, Qd2, O-O-O, and f4.

## Anti-Sicilian Systems

### Alapin (2.c3)
White aims for d4 with a solid centre. Leads to IQP positions after 2...d5 3.exd5 Qxd5 4.d4. See [Middlegame — Pawn Structures](../../middlegame/pawn-structures.md).

### Grand Prix Attack (2.Nc3 Nc6 3.f4)
Straightforward kingside attack. f4 supports e5 and aims to crack open the kingside. Popular at club level.

### Closed Sicilian (2.Nc3, 3.g3)
White fianchettoes and plays a slow, manoeuvring game. Avoids open Sicilian theory entirely.

### Moscow Variation (3.Bb5+)
```
1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.Bb5+
```
A trendy anti-Sicilian at the top level. Disrupts Black's development.
