# Petrov's Defense (Russian Game)

**1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nf6**

A solid, symmetrical response. Instead of defending e5, Black counter-attacks e4. Leads to quiet, strategic positions where endgame technique matters most.

**See also:** [Ruy Lopez](ruy-lopez.md) | [Four Knights](four-knights.md) | [Endgames — Rook Endings](../../endgames/rook-endings.md)

## Main Line

```
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nf6 3.Nxe5 d6 4.Nf3 Nxe4 5.d4 d5 6.Bd3 Nc6 7.O-O Be7 8.c4 Nb4 9.Be2 O-O 10.Nc3 Bf5
```

### Critical Warning

After 3.Nxe5, Black must **not** play 3...Nxe4?? — White wins with 4.Qe2! exploiting the pin. The correct move is **3...d6** first.

### Strategic Ideas

| White | Black |
|-------|-------|
| Seize a small, lasting advantage | Aim for solid equality |
| d4, c4 — use the space advantage | Symmetrical structure, neutralise White's initiative |
| The position often resembles a QGD | Reach a drawable endgame or play for small advantages |

### Typical Pawn Structure

Symmetric centre (d4/d5) is very common. The position often resembles a [Queen's Gambit Declined](../closed-games/qgd.md) structure.

### The Cochrane Gambit

```
3.Nxe5 d6 4.Nxf7!? Kxf7
```

A rare but fun attacking try — White sacrifices a knight to expose Black's king. Objectively dubious but dangerous in practice.

## Famous Practitioners

Vladimir Kramnik, Viswanathan Anand, Fabiano Caruana, Sergey Karjakin.

## Who Should Play It

Players who want a rock-solid equalising system as Black. Suits technical players comfortable in endgames. Not for those seeking dynamic, unbalanced positions.
