# Philidor Defense

**1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 d6**

Named after François-André Danican Philidor, the greatest player of the 18th century. Black solidly defends e5 with ...d6 but blocks the dark-squared bishop. A defensive but resilient system.

## Main Line

```
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 Nf6 (or 3...exd4) 4.Nc3 Nbd7 5.Bc4 Be7 6.O-O O-O
```

### Strategic Ideas

| White | Black |
|-------|-------|
| Space advantage with e4 + d4 | Solid but cramped position |
| Develop naturally and look for f4 or dxe5 breaks | Plan ...c6 and ...d5 to challenge the centre |
| The bishop on c4 eyes f7 | ...Nf8–g6 to reinforce the kingside |

### Typical Pawn Structure

White maintains e4/d4 vs Black's e5/d6. Black's position is solid but passive. The key question is whether Black can achieve ...d5 to free the position.

---

## Philidor Countergambit (3...f5?!)

```
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 f5?!
```

Ambitious but risky. Black immediately challenges e4 but weakens the kingside. White can exploit with 4.dxe5 fxe4 5.Ng5.

## Hanham Variation (3...Nd7)

```
1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 Nd7
```

A more modern, flexible approach. Black avoids the pin (Bg5) and keeps options for ...Be7 or ...g6 setups.

---

## Historical Significance

The Philidor Defense features in the most famous teaching game in chess history: [The Opera Game](../../famous-games/opera-game.md) (Morphy vs Duke of Brunswick & Count Isouard, 1858). Morphy's victory demonstrated textbook development principles against the passive Philidor setup.

## Famous Practitioners

Philidor (18th century), Etienne Bacrot, various modern GMs as a surprise weapon.

## Who Should Play It

Players who want a solid, low-maintenance defence against 1.e4. Less theoretical than the [Ruy Lopez](ruy-lopez.md) or [Italian](italian-game.md). Suits those who prefer defence and counter-punching.
