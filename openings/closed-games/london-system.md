# London System

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4** (or 1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4)

A popular "system" opening — White plays the same setup regardless of Black's response. The bishop goes to f4 early, before playing e3. The London has become extremely popular at all levels due to its simplicity and reliability.

## Typical Setup

```
1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nd2 e6 6.Ngf3 Bd6 7.Bg3
```

White's ideal formation: pawns on d4, e3, c3; bishop on f4 (or g3 after ...Bd6); knights on f3 and d2; bishop on d3 (or e2).

### Strategic Ideas

| White | Black |
|-------|-------|
| Solid, low-risk setup | Challenge with ...c5 to open the centre |
| Bf4 controls the h2–b8 diagonal | ...Qb6 pressures b2 and d4 |
| Play on the kingside: Ne5 is a key manoeuvre | Active piece play to exploit White's passivity |
| h3, g4 possible for a slow kingside attack | ...Nh5 to challenge the Bf4 bishop |

### Key Manoeuvres

1. **Ne5** — the London's signature move. The knight on e5 is a powerful outpost.
2. **Bg3** — retreating after ...Bd6 to avoid the trade. White keeps the dark-squared bishop.
3. **f3 + e4** — a typical pawn break to open the centre when ready.

---

## Against Different Setups

### vs King's Indian Setup (...g6, ...Bg7)
White can maintain the London setup. The Bf4 is well-placed against the fianchetto since it controls key dark squares.

### vs ...c5 + ...Qb6
Black's most testing approach. ...Qb6 attacks b2 and d4 simultaneously, putting White under early pressure.

---

## Strengths and Weaknesses

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
|-----------|------------|
| Easy to learn — same setup every game | Can be passive if Black knows the antidotes |
| Avoids sharp theoretical battles | Limited winning chances against well-prepared opponents |
| Solid; hard to lose | e3 locks in the dark-squared bishop somewhat |
| Popular at all levels including top GM play | Black has several paths to comfortable equality |

## Famous Practitioners

Magnus Carlsen (popularised it at the top level), Gata Kamsky, Levon Aronian, Hikaru Nakamura.

## Who Should Play It

Players at any level who want a reliable, low-maintenance system against 1...d5 and 1...Nf6. Excellent for those who prefer understanding over memorisation. The London lets you focus on middlegame plans rather than opening theory.
